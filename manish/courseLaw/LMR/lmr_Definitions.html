CONTRACT LAW MNEMONICS

1. COALL = The required elements of a valid contract 

  • Consideration
  • Offer
  • Acceptance
  • Legal capacity
  • Legal purpose

2. MYLEG = Contracts that require a writing under the Statute of Frauds

  • contracts for Marriage
  • contracts that necessarily take over a Year from execution to complete
  • contracts for conveyance of an interest in Land
  • contracts by an Executor of an estate
  • contracts for Guarantee of a debt

[Note that the mnemonic is MYLEG and not MYLEGS because the last element, Sales of goods, is now controlled by UCC section 2-201 and the Statute of Frauds no longer has any relevance.]

3. CCCC = Consequential Damages Elements

  • lost profits were Contemplated by parties at time of formation
  • lost profits are Certain in amount
  • lost profits were Caused by the breach
  • lost profits Couldn't be avoided

4. DAM FOIL = Parole Evidence Rule exceptions 

  • to show contract was result of Duress
  • to clarify Ambiguity
  • to show Mistake
  • to prove Fraud
  • to prove an Oral condition precedent
  • to show Illegality of purpose
  • to show Lack of consideration

Types of Contracts

After above discussion, try to find out the type of contract question, there are 6 types of contract questions and / or mixes of all 6 types

Type 1: Formation of Contracts - Questions?

If questions says, a valid contract is formed, then don't discuss formation of contract.

Raise issues of whether contract ever formed at all.
Was there an OFFER?
ACCEPTANCE?
Manifestation of INTENT?
Was the contract for a LEGAL PURPOSE?
Supported by CONSIDERATION?
Between parties with LEGAL Capacity?

For formation of contract, go through each communication between the parties one by one addressing these issues in this order.

  1. Was there a valid OFFER?
    1. Was there manifestation of INTENT?
    2. Not effectively REVOKED?
    3. A GENERAL offer?
  2. Was there an ACCEPTANCE of an offer?
    1. Not REJECTED by offeree?
    2. A TIMELY acceptance?
    3. MIRROR IMAGE RULE (CL) ?
    4. Accepted by SHIPMENT (UCC) ?
  3. What were the TERMS of the contract?
    1. Is it unequivocally clear the offer is for a UNILATERAL contact?
    2. Certain as to TERMS?
    3. A DIVISIBILE contract (UCC) ?
    4. A PRODUCTION or REQUIREMENTS contract (UCC) ?
    5. Is timely performance an express condition or mere covenant?
    6. Is personal satisfaction an express condition or is there just a promise to render reasonably satisfactory performance (an implied condition of every contract) ?
  4. Supported by CONSIDERATION? (Watch for MODIFICATION and ILLUSIONARY PROMISE problems)
  5. Was the contract for a LEGAL PURPOSE? (Omit if not an issue)
  6. Between parties with LEGAL CAPACITY? (Watch for MINORS)

Follow this pattern:

  1. First look at each communication chronologically as a separate issue to see if it is an offer.
  2. Second, after you find an offer, examine each following communication as a separate issue looking for an acceptance. It often is a rejection and counter-offer.
  3. Watch out for and apply the mailbox rules to revocations and rejections by mail/fax.
  4. When you get a valid acceptance to a valid offer, BINGO !, you have a contract. So the very next single issue is to determine its terms.
  5. Don't discuss consideration, legal purpose or legal capacity of the parties unless the facts call for discussion.
    1. Remember that consideration is a bargained for exchange.
    2. No moral obligation
    3. No prior consideration
    4. No promise qualified by a future decision to perform.

Type 2: Term Disputes

What were the terms of the contract?
Sufficiently CERTAIN that a court can enforce?
Was there a MODIFICATION supported by consideration?
Is a term or promised performance a MATERIAL CONDITION?
Is a given term or promise INCLUDED or EXCLUDED from the agreement?

These raise the questions "What were the terms of the contract?" If FORMATION is in doubt, then the TERMS are probably in dispute as well. Look at the total communication and course of dealing between the parties and address these issues:

  1. Was the agreement CERTAIN that a court can enforce it?
    1. Remember for UCC only the quantity and parties must be certain.
    2. Otherwise, were the subject matter, time, price, quantity and parties certain?
  2. Was there a MODIFICATION that was ineffective because unsupported by consideration? Remember this does not apply the UCC question!
  3. Does the PAROLE EVIDENCE RULE exclude extrinsic evidence? (Remember the mnemonic DAM FOIL!)

There are three situations where the parties dispute the terms. Usually this is only one issue and it will be one of the following:

  1. Is the contract too uncertain to be enforced?
    1. No quantity stated? (Not a UCC requirements or production contract)
    2. Parties uncertain?
    3. Uncertain as to time, price, subject matter? (Not UCC where gap fillers apply)

  2. Is the modification supported by adequate consideration?
    1. Prior Obligation?
    2. This does not apply to UCC where no considerations is needed?

  3. Does the parole evidence rule bar extrinsic evidence of additional terms?
    1. Merger clause?
    2. Contract complete within the four corners of the document?
    3. Remember the exceptions (mnemonic = DAM FOIL)

  4. Was time performance an EXPRESS CONDITION (material condition) or just a COVENANT (not a material condition)?
  5. Was personal satisfaction an EXPRESS CONDITION (a material condition that the buyer must actually be satisfied) or just a COVENANT (an expression of the implied condition inherent in all contracts that performance must be "reasonably satisfactory")?

Type 3: Anticipatory Breach Questions.

Focus on the issue of who breached first?
Anticipatory Breach?
Major or Minor Breach?
Waiver?

These raise the questions, "Who breached first?" There are two kinds of breach, either breaches in fact or anticipatory repudiation.

A breach in fact means that party did not perform at the time they were supposed to perform.

An anticipatory repudiation means that a party clearly states an intent or inability to perform at some time in the future.

For anticipatory breach questions look at each communication between the parties one by one until you find the first clear breach. Remember, only one party can breach a contract at a time. Once the first party breaches, the other party CANNOT breach unless the contract is "reformed" by waiver or accord of a good faith dispute.

  1. Was there an ANTICIPATORY REPUDIATION - a clear statement of intent not to perform a future obligation?
  2. Was there a WAIVER of the anticipatory breach?
  3. Was there an ACCORD AND SATISFACTION of a good faith dispute?
  4. Was timely performance an express condition or mere covenant?
  5. Was personal satisfaction an express condition or is there just a promise to render reasonably satisfactory performance (an implied condition of every contract)?

These situations are where one party says, "I don't think I can meet the deadline." Then the other party says, "Oh yeah? Well then the deal's off. Follow this pattern:

  1. Look at each communication as a separate issue to see if it is a clear statement of intent not to perform at a future time. If a party says they don't think they will be able to perform in the future, that is expression of a doubt and not an expression of an intent.
  2. The first party to clearly state an intent to not perform is the breaching party.
  3. When a party breaches the next issue is the effect of the anticipatory breach. 1) excuses the non breaching party from performance and it 2) accelerates the performance obligation of the breaching party.
  4. The next issue is whether the non-breaching party waives the breach by allowing the breaching party to continue performance under the contract.

Type 4: Defense Questions

Focus on defense issues:
Statute of Frauds issue?
Lack of consideration?
Illegal purpose?
Rights of underage party?
Fraud? Duress?

These raise the following issues:

  1. Does the STATUTE OF FRAUDS (OR UCC 2 - 201 in the case of a sale of goods) require writings?
    1. Was the need for writing satisfied?
  2. Did the contract (or modification if not a UCC question) lack CONSIDERATION?
  3. Was the contract for an ILLEGAL purpose (or unconscionable / adhesion contract) ?
  4. Was the contract voidable by a MINOR or other party lacking capacity?
  5. Was the contract void because it was the product of FRAUD or DURESS?
  6. Was the contract void because of MUTUAL MISTAKE?
  7. Was the contract void because of performance was IMPOSSIBLE or FRUSTRATION OF PURPOSE?

If there is a breach, go through each of the breaching party's defenses as separate issues:

  1. First, was there an insufficient writing to satisffy the Statute of Frauds?
  2. Lack of consideration? Prior obligation? Moral obligation? Prior consideration? Gift promise?
  3. Illegal purpose? Does in pare delicto apply?
  4. Breaching party a minor or incapacitated? But, did they repudiate within time after removal of incapacity?
  5. Contract induced by fraud, duress, or because of mistake? Did the non-breaching party know, or should they have known that the other party was mistaken?
  6. Performance impossible or purpose frustrated? Was it absolutely impossible? These situations violate IMPLIED MATERIAL CONDITIONS of the contract, causing it to fail.

Specific Performance

  2. What are the UCC remedies (COVER, REJECT, etc)?
  3. Did the non-breaching party FAIL TO MITIGATE damages?
  4. Was there SUBSTANTIAL PERFORMANCE (a MINOR breach) or MAJOR breach?
  5. Can a party obtain SPECIFIC PERFORMANCE?
  6. Can the non breaching party get CONSEQUENTIAL DAMAGES (LOST PROFITS)?

    2. Remember the mnemonic CCCC.
    3. Were lost profits Contemplated by the parties at time of execuation?
    4. Were lost profits Caused by the breach?
    5. Were lost profits Certain as to amounts?

  7. Do the SAVING DOCTRINES of the unilateral contract apply?
  8. If there is NO VALID ENFORCEABLE CONTRACT can the parties obtain EQUITABLE RELIEF?

Each of the remedies is a separtate issue. Only discuss the issues that apply.

  1. If there was a valid contract, was there substantial performance? Benefit of bargain.
  2. Can the plaintiff obtain specific performance? Unique goods?
  3. Can the contract be enforced based on promissory estoppel? Injustice resulting?
  4. Can the party obtain rescission and restitution? Return to status quo?
  5. Should the damages be reduced because of a failure to mitigate?
  6. Are consequential damages called for? Cite Hadly v Baxendale, and the mnemonic is CCCC.
  7. If there is NO VALID, ENFORCEABLE CONTRACT what EQUITABLE remedies is available to the parties?

A2. STATUTE OF FRAUDS

Mnemonic for Statute of Frauds is MYLEG. Contracts can be written if they are

  1. a contract for marriage
  2. a contract that must take more than a year to complete (from date of execution)
  3. a contract for conveyence of an interest in Land (e.g rental agreements, sales, easements)
  4. a contract by an Executor of the estate of a deceased, and
  5. a contract by a Gurantor of a debt

A5. BREACH

1. ANTICIPATORY BREACH?

Note: If the non-breaching party allows the breaching party to continue performance after the anticipatory breach, either discuss WAIVER OF THE BREACH as an accompanying issue (see below) or else consider skipping this issue altogether.

Under contract law an ANTICIPATORY REPUDIATION is a clear statement of intent or other clear indication by a party that they will not perform a future obligation. An anticipatory repudiation is a MAJOR breach. The effect is to 1) excuse the other party from performance and 2) accelerate all future obligations of the breaching party to the present.

If a party has a REASONABLE BASIS to believe the other party may not perform future contractual duties, the party may demand REASONABLE ASSURANCES and REFUSE TO PERFORM until they are provided. Reasonable assurances generally means a financial gurantee or payment into escrow.

If reasonable assurances are properly demanded and not provided, the failure to respond constitutes an anticipatory breach.

Here there was (not) an anticipatory breach because the party said "...." and that (did not) make it clear they were not going to perform. That did (not) form a reasonable basis for (the other party) to demand reasonable assurances because ... Therefore....

2. WAIVER of the breach?

Note: This is an issue when there is an anticipatory breach or other major breach but afterward the non-breaching party lets the breaching party resume or continue performance.

Under contract law if a non-breaching party lets the breaching party continue performance after an anticipatory breach or other major breach it WAIVES THE BREACH (technically this is called an ELECTION). The waiver cannot be revoked, and the non-breaching party is forever ESTOPPED from citing the breach as major and can only cite it as a minor breach.

5. ACCORD AND SATISFACTION?

Under contract law an ACCORD AND SATISFACTION is an agreement by the contract parties to settle a reasonable and good faith claim of breach by one of the parties. It is binding on both parties.

The knowing acceptance by one party of any payment or other offer to settle a contract dispute by the other party is generally binding on both parties, even if the party accepting payment says they do so "under protest" or "reserving their claim" or some statement of that type.

A6. THIRD PARTY BENEFICIARIES

1. THIRD PARTY BENEFICIARY contract?

Under contract law a THIRD PARTY BENEFICIARY contract is one betwen a promisee who promises to give something of value to a promisor in exchange for the promisor's promise to do something to benefit a third party, the INTENDED THIRD PARTY BENEFICIARY. If the purpose of the promisee are gratuitous, the third party beneficiary is called a DONEE BENEFICARY. If the promisee is acting to satisfy a legal obligation, the third party beneficiary is called a CREDITOR BENEFICIARY.

Promisors that breach third party beneficiary contracts are legally liable to both the promisees and all intended beneficiaries. Promisees that breach third party beneficiary contracts are legally liable to promisors and creditor beneficiaries but NOT to donee beneficiaries.

Under early common law parties that breached third party beneficiary contracts were only legally liable to intended beneficiaries that were VESTED because they had been aware of the existence of the contract and had relied on it before the breach occured. Modernly that requirement has been adandoned.

Donee beneficiaries can only seek a remedy against breaching promisees in EQUITY based on a claim of DETRIMENTAL RELIANCE.

When contrats would incidentally benefit third parties, but their benefit was not the purpose of the contracts, the third parties are called INCIDENTAL BENEFICIARIES. Parties that breach contracts have no liability to incidental beneficiaries in law or equity.

Note: Third party questions require that you identify the parties and explain all of the rights and liabilites each party has against th eother parties. That usually takes so much time that there is little time left for anything else.

2. ASSIGNMENT of contract benefits?

Under contract law promisees can ASSIGN the benefits they are promised under a contrat to third parties called ASSIGNEES. Promisees who assign contract benefits are called ASSIGNORS.

Contract promises that they "will not be assigned" are "mere covenants" and not express material conditions unless the contract language is unequivocally clear that the contract parties intended for non-assignment to be an express material condition. Contract assginments in violation of express material conditions are void ab initio. Contract assignments in breach of mere covenants are minor breaches and promisors have a right to an award of damages against the promisees if any can be proven.

Contract assignments are only EFFECTIVE if assignors clearly express their intent to assign the contracts, and notice of the assignments have been given to the promisors.

If assignments are for gratuitous purposes the assignees are called DONEE ASSIGNEES and the assignments are LEGALLY REVOCABLE by the assignors untill they have been stated by assignors in writing OR a token chose has been delivered. Revocation of a gratuitous assignment may be estopped (in equity) based on a claim of detrimental reliance Assignment to a donee assignee is a gift, no contract is created between the assignor and the assignee, and the assignment does not imply any warranties by the assignor.

If assignments are made to satisfy legal obligations the assignees are called CREDITOR ASSIGNEES, the assignments create legal contracts between the assignees and the assignors (promisees), and they are IRREVOCABLE. An assignment to a creditor assignee implies warranties by the assignor 1) that the rights assigne exists, 2) that the promisee/assignor has the authority to assign them to the assignee, and 3) all documents provided to the assignee by the assignor are bone fide. The assignee may seek legal damages from the assignor for a breach of those warranties.

Promisors who breach contracts after they have been effectively assigned (and the assignment has not been effectively revoked) are only legally liable to the assignees and are NOT legally liable to the assignors (original promisees). And the promisors can assert any legal or equitable defence against assignees that could have been asserted against the promisees but for the assignment.

Promisees (assignors) are not released from contract obligations by an assignent and if they breach the contracts after they assign them they are legally liable to promisors and creditor assignees but NOT to donee assignees. And the promisees can assert any legal or equitable defense against promisors and assignees that could have been asserted against promisors alone but for the assignment.

Donee assignees can only seek a remey against breaching promisees in EQUITY based on a claim of DETRIMENTAL RELIANCE.

3. MODIFICATION OF CONTRACT AFTER ASSIGNMENT?

If a promisor and promisee agree to modify a contract after the promisee has assigned the contract rights to an assignee, the modification agreement is binding on the assignee IF the contract has not yet been fully performd AND the modification is made in good faith in accordance with reasonable commercial standards. The assignee acquires corresponding rights under the modified contract.

Note, for example: Suppose promisee was to complete performance and be paid on 6/1, and promisee assigned the rights to be paid to assignee. Assignee had a right to be paid on 6/1. But if promisee and promisor agree to delay completion to 7/1 (perhaps because of material shortages) that is binding on assignee. Now assignee has a right to be paid on 7/1 and not on 6/1.

4. DELEGATION of contract duties?

Under Contract law promisors may DELEGATE the duties they have promised to perform under a contract to third parties called DELEGATEES. Promisors who assign conctract duties are called DELEGATORS.

Contracts that necessarily require the personal services of the promisors, and that cannot feasibly be performed by anyone else, are subject to implied material conditions that the promisors cannot delegate contract duties. Otherwise, express contract promises that they "will not be assigned" are "mere covenants" and not express material conditions unless the contract language is unequivocally clear that the contract parties intended for personal performance of contract duties by the promisor to be an express material condition.

Delegations of contract duties in violation of express or implied material conditions are void ab initio. Delegations of contract duties in breach of mere covenants are minor breaches and promisors have a right to an award of damages if any can be proven.

Contract delegations are only EFFECTIVE when the delegatee agrees to perform the duties of the promisor/delegator. No notice to the promisee is required.

Delegatees that GRATUITOUSLY agree to perform the duties of delegators/promisors are just volunteers, and they may be called VOLUNTEER DELEGATEES. Volunteer delegatees have no legal duty to perform.

Delegetees that agree to perform the duties of delegators/promisors in exchange FOR CONSIDERATION are legally bound in a DELEGATION CONTRACT with the delegator promisor and may be called CONTRACTED DELEGATEES. Contracted delegatees have a legal duty to perform.

Delegation contracts between delegators and contracted delegatees are third party beneficiary contracts because the promisee (in the original contract) is the intended beneficiary of the contract between the delegator and the contracted delegatee.

If delegatees fail to perform the delegator/promisor is always liable to the promisee, regardless whether the delegatee is a volunteer or a contracted delegatee.

If volunteer delegatees fail to perform they have no legal duty to anyone. Delegators (promisors) and promisees can only seek a remedy against volunteer delegatees in EQUITY based on claim of detrimental reliance.

If contracted delegatees fail to perform they are legally liable to both the delegators and the promisees of the original contract. The action of a promisee against a contracted delegatee is brought as a third party beneficiary under contract between the delegator and the contracted delegatee contract. Contracted delegatees can assert any defense against promisees that they could assert against delegators, and any defenses the delegators could assert against the promisees.

Here .. because ... Therefore..

Note: Like third party beneficiary questions, assignment and delegation questions require that you identify the parties, and explain all of the rights and liabilites each party has against the other parties. That usually takes so much time that there is little time left for anything else.

A7. REMEDIES

1. COMMON LAW REMEDY of the NON-BREACHING PARTY?

Under common law the non-breaching party has a right to COMPENSATORY DAMAGES, award of a money judgement calculated as the sum of:

  1. RELIANCE DAMAGES, expenses incurred in reliance upon the promise of the breaching party,
  2. EXPECTATION DAMAGES, the loss of expected benefits caused by the breach,
  3. INCIDENTAL DAMAGES, expenses incurred after the breach as a result (e.g. storage charges),
  4. CONSEQUENTIAL DAMAGES, lost profits on collateral contracts with third parties that could not be performed because of the breach.

Under Hadley v Baxendale, CONSEQUENTIAL DAMAGES can only be awarded if, at the time the original contract was executed, the breaching party knew of or otherwise could have contemplated the non-breaching party would enter into collateral contracts and would lose profits on them as a result of a contract breach.

The burden is on a non-breaching party to prove the damages suffered with CERTAINITY, to prove they were CAUSED BY THE BREACH, and that they COULD NOT BE AVOIDED after the breach occured. By implication this gives the non-breaching party a duty to mitigate damages.

As an alternative to requesting a money judgement based on damages the non breaching party has a legal right to demand LEGAL RESTITUTION, a money judgement calculated as the value of the benefits the breaching party received as a result of the non-breaching party's contract performance, up to the amount promised under the contract terms.

A non-breaching buyer of unique property may ask the Court (in equity) to award SPECIFIC PERFORMANCE on a claim that award of a money judgement would be an inadequate remedy. Specific performance of services by a natural person cannot be awarded by the Court because it violates the 13th Amendment. And there is no "right" to specific performance.

2. COMMON LAW REMEDY of the BREACHING PARTY?

Under contract law a breaching party that has not committed a major breach will be deemed to have SUBSTANTIALLY PERFORMED and has a right to receive all benefits and payments promised under the contract provisions less an OFFSET for the damages (or legal restitution) awarded to the non-breaching party.

A party that has committed a major breach has no legal right to any remedy and must plead in equity. A Court of equity may award the breaching party EQUITABLE RESTITUTION for amounts paid and benefits conferred to the extent they exceed damages awarded to the non-breaching party to PREVENT UNJUST ENRICHMENT or FRUSTRATION OF REASONABLE EXPECTATIONS based on a claim of implied-in-law contract.

3. LIQUIDATED DAMAGES?

Under contract law a LIQUIDATED DAMAGES CLAUSE will be binding if it was a reasonable provision at the time the contract was executed, because it was foreseeable at the time the contract was executed a breach would cause damages that were not foreseeable in amount, AND it produces a reasonable remedy after the contract is breached. In this event the sole remedy for the non-breaching party is award of a money judgement in the amount specified in the contract.

Since a money judgement often is not an adequate remedy for a non-breaching buyer of unique property, liquidatd daages clauses generally will not produce a reasonable remedy and will not be binding in that situation.

Here... because ... therefore...

4. IMPLIED-IN-LAW CONTRACT?

IMPLIED-IN-LAW CONTRACT is an equitable theory or "cause of action" that may be pled by a movant when a contract fails or otherwise there is no valid enforceable contract which will provide a remedy. The Court has discretion to provide EQUITABLE RESTITUTION when there is no valid, legally enforceable contract if the movant acted to convey benefits to the responding party with a reasonable expectaion of being compensated in return.

The Court may either award the amount necessary to prevent an unjust enrichment to the respondent, or the amount necessary to prevent frustration of the reasonable expectations of the movant.

Note: This argument should only be raised when no contract is otherwise enforceable at law. Perhaps there was a contract that violated the Statute of Frauds, there was a major breach, or the respondent lacked legal capacity. This equitable theory requires that the movant reasonably expected to be paid for services rendered.

5. PROMISSORY ESTOPPEL?

PROMISSORY ESTOPPEL is an equitable theory or "cause of action" that may be pled by a movant when there is no valid, legally enforceable contract or other legal cause of action but the movant has acted in reasonable reliance on a promise by the respondent, the respoondent intended to induce the movant to rely on the promise, and justice demands that the promise be enforced to some degree.

The Court may either estop the respondent from reneging on the promise or award the movant a money judgement in EQUITABLE RESTITUTION to prevent the respondent from reaping an unjust enrichment or to compensate the movant for injures suffered or to prevent frustration of reasonable expectations.

Here... because ... therefore...

Often the promise is a gift promise or an assurance the respondent will not assert some legal defense or institute some legal action.

1.

Lucy owned a rent house. She advertised in the newspaper to find a renter, citing rent of $700 a month.

Homer saw the ad and called Lucy at 9:00 a.m. Homer said, “"I saw your ad and accept your offer! I will be right there to pay you. I have to see the house, but would you consider $550?”

Lucy said, “It's a deal. I will rent to you for $550. This is a firm offer.” Homer said he would be there in one hour to see the house.

Homer went 100 miles an hour to see the house, sideswiping Victoria. Victoria crashed and died along with her 7-month fetus.

At 3:00 p.m. Homer arrived, five hours late, ran up to Lucy and said, “I unequivocally accept. Here is my $550.”

Lucy said, “I have decided not to rent to you.” Discuss the rights of Homer and Lucy's defenses.

HOMER VS LUCY

The rights and remedies of the parties depend on whether or not there was a valid contract. A contract is a promise or set of promises the performance of which the law will recognize as a duty and for which the law will provide a remedy.

1. UCC?

UCC Article 2 governs contracts for sale of GOODS, movable things at the time of identification to the contract. Otherwise, only COMMON LAW governs the contract.

Here the contract does not concern a sale of movable things because it is for the "rent" of a "house".

Therefore, only COMMON LAW principles govern this contract.

2. Is a WRITING REQUIRED?

Under the STATUTE OF FRAUDS certain types of contract must be in writing to be enforced. A contract for conveyance of an interest in LAND often requires a writing, but not leaseholds of a year or less.

Here the question involves conveyance of an interest in land because the alleged agreement was to rent a "house", but this appears to be a month-month rental.

Therefore, a writing would not generally be required.

3. Was the ADVERTISEMENT an OFFER?

Under contract law an OFFER is a manifestation of present intent to enter into a bargain communicated to the offeree and sufficiently specific that an observer would reasonably believe assent would form a bargain. Advertisements are generally not offers because they usually fail to identity the parties or the quantity being offered.

Here the advertisement was not specific as to the parties because it did not guarantee that Lucy would rent to the first person to respond.

Therefore, the advertisement was not an offer.

4. Was the 9:00 statement by Homer an OFFER?

Offer is defined supra

Here the statement by Homer did not appear to manifest intent to enter into a bargain because even though he said, "I accept" he added that he "had to see the house first". No reasonable person would believe assent to that statement would form a bargain because it was clear he wanted to "see the house first".

Therefore the statement was not an offer.

5. Was the 9.00 response by Lucy an OFFER?

Offer is defined supra

Here the statement by Lucy did manifest intent to enter into a bargain because she said "It's a deal, I will rent to you for $550." A reaonable person would believe assent to that statment would form a bargain.

Therefore that statement was an offer.

6. Did Homer have a valid OPTION?

Offer is defined supra. Under common law an OPTION is a contractual agreement that an offeror will not revoke an offer for a specific period of time in exchange for CONSIDERATION from the offeree. CONSIDERATION is an exchange of promises posing legal detriment such that the law deems it sufficient to support an agreement.

Here Lucy promised to give a "firm offer", but Homer gave no promise or value in exchange. While the UCC provides for "firm offers" from merchants, the UCC does not govern here. Therefore, Lucy received no legal consideration in exchange for her promise to give a "firm offer" and her promise cannot be enforced against her as an option contract.

Therefore, Lucy's offer did not create an option contract, and she could revoke her offer at any time.

7. Did Homer effectively ACCEPT at 3.00?

At common law an acceptance must be an unequivocal assent to the terms of an offer, dispatched to the offeror in the manner required, or if no manner is required in the offer, by the means by which the offer was dispatched or some faster means. An offer must be accepted before it LAPSES. Unless there is an option, an offer lapses in the time stated or if no time stated within a reasonable time after the offer is made.

Here the reasonable time for acceptance of Lucy's offer is defined by the understanding that Homer would view the house in only one hour at "10:00". That understanding established the "reasonable time" within which Homer could accept the offer. But Homer was "five hours late," as Lucy's offer had already lapsed when he arrived. Homer's attempt to unequivocally accept Lucy's 9:00 offer by saying "I unequivocally accept" was ineffective because he could not accept an offer that had already lapsed.

Therefore, Homer's attempt to accept was ineffective.

[Explanation: This question is a typical FORMATION question because it goes through a series of communications between the parties. When you see messages going back and forth, it is a FORMATION question, so analyze each message.

Start with an INTRODUCTORY DEFINITION of a contract. Then chronologically analyze each communication to see if it is an OFFER. Once there is an offer, the next issue is whether the offer is ACCEPTED.

If there is offer and an acceptance a contract forms and the next issue is the TERMS of the contract.

After the contract terms are discussed the next issue is whether there has been a BREACH. And if there is a berach, the issues are WHO BREACHED and the REMEDIES of the parties.]

Bob told Al that he could not make a tuxedo so fast and suggested instead that Al buy a ready- made tuxedo that he had in stock in exactly Al's size. Al agreed and said he would be in to pick it up later that week. Price was not discussed.

Bob took in the pants and sent Al a note that said, "I took in the pants and it should fit you like a glove. With alterations the price is now $750.

Al was furious.

Carla's wedding was suddenly called off. Al never called Bob and never went to get the tuxedo. Bob was furious.

Does Bob have a right to be paid for the tuxedo? What are Al's defenses?

BOB v. AL

The rights and remedies of the parties here depend on whether there was a valid contract. A CONTRACT is a promise or set of promises the performance of which the law will recognize as a duty and for the breach of which the law will provide a remedy.

1. UCC?

Article 2 of the UCC governs contracts for the sale of GOODS, movable things at the time of identification to the contract.

Here the contract is for sale of a movable thing because it was for sale of a "tuxedo".

Therefore this was a contract for a sale of goods and the UCC governs.

2. MERCHANTS?

Under the UCC MERCHANTS are people who deal in the goods or hold themselves out by occupation as knowledgeable about the goods in a contract.

Here Bob deals in tuxedos because he has a clothing store. Al may have "bought a lot of clothes" but there is no evidence he deals in tuxedos or hold himself out by occupation as being particularly knowledgeable about them.

Therefore Bob is a merchant and Al is not.

3. Is a WRITING NEEDED?

Under UCC 2-201 a contract for the sale of GOODS over $500 requires a writing. This limit is being raised to $5,000 but many States are still using the $500 limit. Under the UCC a contract between MERCHANTS may satisfy the need for a writing against both parties if there is a sales confirmation indicating the quantity sent to one party by the other and the receiving party does not object to the representation of a contract.

Further, under the UCC there is no need for a writing if goods are custom made, the person to be bound acknowledges the contract in a legal setting or where a party has accepted payment or goods.

Here the goods are over $500 because the tuxedo costs "$519.95" and Al is not a merchant as shown above.

Further, this was a "ready-made" tuxedo, not custom made, and Al never acknowledged the contract in a legal setting or accepted the goods.

Therefore a writing signed by Al is needed if the contract is to be enforced against him by Bob.

3.

Groucho entered into a contract with new car dealer Harpo to buy a new Dodge for his brother Chico on his birthday. The agreed sales price was $25,000. Harpo promised the new car would be delivered by Chico's birthday within two weeks. Chico was ecstatic. Groucho made a down payment of $2,000 and promised to pay an additional amount of $23,000 for the car over a five- year period.

Harpo made a deal with another dealer, Swifty. Swifty agreed to deliver a Dodge of the type specified on time for Chico's birthday in exchange for an immediate payment of $20,000.

Swifty became insolvent, its inventory was seized and it did not deliver the Dodge. Harpo blames Swifty.
Chico was upset he didn't get the car he expected.
What are the rights, remedies and defenses of Groucho, Harpo, Chico and Swifty?

The rights and remedies of the parties here depend on whether there was a valid contract. A CONTRACT is a promise or set of promises the performance of which the law will recognize as a duty and for the breach of which the law will provide a remedy.

1. UCC?

UCC Article 2 governs the contracts for the sale of GOODS, movable things at the time of identification to the contract.

Here the question involves a sale of a "car" and it is movable because it was to be delivered.

Therefore, the UCC applies here.

GROUCHO v. HARPO

2. Groucho's rights under a THIRD-PARTY BENEFICIARY CONTRACT?

Under contract law a THIRD-PARTY BENEFICIARY CONTRACT is one with main purpose of providing a benefit to a thrid party. The contract can be enforced by the original party, the promisee, or by a vested intended beneficiary.

A beneficiary may be a DONEE (of a gift) or a CREDITOR (payment of debt) beneficiary. The beneficiary vests upon becoming aware of and changing position in reliance on the contract.

Here the purpose of the contract is to benefit Chico because he was to get a "new Dodge" as a "birthday present." Therefore this is a third-party benefit contract. Chico is intended DONEE beneficiary because it is a "birthday present".

Groucho is the promisee because Harpo promised to "deliver" the car in "two weeks". Harpo is the promisor because he has promised to deliver the car to Chico.

Therefore, this is a third-party beneficiary contract, and Groucho as promisee can enforce it.

3. Groucho's REMEDIES?

Under the PERFECT TENDER RULE of the UCC a non-breaching buyer in a non-delivery situation can demand conforming goods, repudiate the contract or by goods to cover and demand the excess of the cover price over the contract price.

Here Harpo has breached because he did not deliver the promised car.

Therefore, Groucho can demand delivery from Harpo or repudiate the contract and demand return of his $2,000. If he covers by buying the car elsewhere he can demand the excess of his cover price, if any, over the contract price.

GROUCHO V. SWIFTY

4. Groucho's rights under the DELEGATION contract?
Under contract law, a DELEGATION is a second contract conveying the duty of performance under the first contract from the original promisor to a delegatee. The promisor in the first contract retains primary liability to the promisee. The delegatee assumes secondary liability to the promisee of the first contract if she expressly accepts the delegation in exchange for consideration, a bargained for exchange of value creating legal liability. In the delegation contract between the promisor / delegator and the delegatee the original promisee is a CREDITOR third-party beneficiary with standing to enforce the contract.

A delegatee can raise all defenses that are available to the promisee in the original contract.

Here there is a delegation contract between Harpo and Swifty supported by consideration because Swifty expressly accepted the duty by saying he "agreed to deliver" in exchange for an "immediate payment of $20,000".

Therefore, Groucho has standing to enforce Swifty's promise to deliver the car as a creditor third party beneficiary.

5. Groucho's REMEDIES against Swifty?
A promisee under a contract can enforce the delegation contract as an intended third-party beneficiary in the same way as the original promisee of the delegation agreement (Harpo).

Here Gruocho's remedies against Swifty are the same as his remedies against Harpo, and Groucho can recover from Swifty any amounts he cannot recover from Harpo.

HARPO V. SWIFTY

6. Harpo's rights under the DELEGATION contract?
Delegation is defined above.

Here the delegation from Harpo to Swifty is a contract between them because Swifty promised "to deliver the car", and it is a legal contract supported by consideration because Swifty received an "immediate payment of "$20,000". Swifty was the promisor and Harp the promisee.

Therefore, Harpo can enforce his contract with Swifty and seek recovery from Swifty for any damages due Groucho and / or Chico.

CHICO V. HARPO

7. Chico's rights as a THIRD-PARTY BENEFICIARY?

Third party beneficiary contracts are defined above. Chico is an intended third-party beneficiary because the known purpose of the original contract was to give him a "birthday present". He vested when he became aware of the original contract and changed position in reliance.

A vested third-party beneficiary can enforce the contract against the promisor in the same manner as the promisee.

Therefore, Chico can enforce the original contract against Harpo in the same manner and extent as Groucho.

8. Chico's REMEDIES

Chico's remedies would be the same as Groucho.

CHICO V. SWIFTY

9. Chico's rights and remedies against Swifty?

Since Swifty agreed to perform the duties of Harpo, Swifty assumed the same liability as Harpo. And since Chico has the same enforcement rights as Groucho, Chico's rights and remedies to enforce the contract against Swifty are the same as against Harpo.

CHICO V. GROUCHO

10. Chico's rights and remedies against Groucho?

A donee beneficiary cannot enforce the promise of a gift against the promisee at law and can only enforce at equity based on promissory estoppel.

Here Groucho entered into the contract with Harpo to give Chico a GIFT, so Chico was a DONEE beneficiary. Where there is no evidence Groucho has breached the contract with Harpo, and even if he did Chico would have no right to enforce the contract against Groucho at law.

Therefore, if Groucho had refused to perform the contract (e.g. had refused to make the car payments) Chico could only plead equity against Groucho based on principals of promissory estoppel.
