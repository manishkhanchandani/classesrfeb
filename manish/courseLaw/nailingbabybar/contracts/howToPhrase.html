HOW TO PHRASE CONTRACT ANSWERS -

Craft your common law contracts answer as follows: 

1. DEFINE "CONTRACT" IN AN INTRODUCTION. You must define "contract" before you can intelligently say anything about contract law. This is your first task.

"The rights and remedies of the parties here depend on whether they had a valid contract. A contract is a promise or set of promises the performance of which the law will recognize as a duty and for the breach of which the law will provide a remedy. 

Some students like to add this additional sentence:

A valid contract is based on an offer, acceptance and consideration for a legal purpose between parties of legal capacity." 

[The mnemonic for this is COALL = Consideration, Offer, Acceptance, Legal purpose, Legal capacity.]

2. SHOW THE UCC DOES NOT APPLY (if necessary). We will discuss the UCC next week. This week we look at contacts that are not covered by the UCC. But in your common law answer your second task is to prove the UCC does not apply. But often this is not necessary. Consider whether the Reader wants you do do this, because otherwise it is just a waste of time.

The common law applies to ALL contracts. NEVER say the common law does not apply to a contract answer. But the UCC modifies the common law for sales of goods only. So the first issue to consider addressing is "Does the UCC apply?"  

The UCC does not govern if the question is NOT about 1) a sale and/or 2) it is NOT a sale of goods. 

GOODS are items that are 1) movable and 2) identifiable to the contract at the time of execution. Goods are goods no matter who sells them. Forget about MERCHANTS. The UCC governs sales of goods between non-merchants the same as it governs sales between merchants!

If a contract mixes goods with services the main purpose determines the proper law for your answer. For example, a contract to buy carpet is a contract for goods, even if the contract includes installation, a service. But a contract to build a house is a contract for a service, even if the contract includes lumber and other materials that are goods. 

These are sales of GOODS: 

  • used cars, boats, stereos, RVs for sale
  • firewood, harvested grain, cows
  • "cut" flowers, "cut" Christmas trees
  • "widgets", "gadgets" and "flibits" (if they are to be delivered)
  • carpet, even if installation is included

These are NOT SALES OF GOODS:  

  • houses, land 
  • boats, cars and furniture for rent
  • standing crops, the right to cut firewood
  • growing flowers, the right to cut down and take a Christmas tree
  • stocks, bonds, contracts, copyrights, patents
  • building structures, even if materials are included
  • leased equipment

3. DETERMINE IF THE STATUTE OF FRAUDS REQUIRES A WRITING. The Statute of Frauds is a major, major issue. Never omit discussion of it. Although the sufficiency of the writing is a DEFENSE issue, the need for a writing goes to the heart of the contract itself. Address the issue right up front. 

NOTE: Some professors demand that all defenses be discussed after all of the plaintiff's causes of action have been discussed. If your professor demands that humor the prof. BUT in many scenarios the defenses drive the entire analysis and ignoring defenses at the beginning IS a disastrous waste of time. If an oral contract is unenforceable at LAW the only remedies of the movant are in EQUITY, and that drives the entire analysis.

The mnemonic for Statute of Frauds is MYLEG. Contracts must be written if they are 

  • a contract for Marriage (e.g. prenuptial agreements), 
  • a contract that must take more than a Year to complete (from the date of execution!), 
  • a contract for conveyance of an interest in Land (e.g. rental agreements, sales, easements),
  • a contract by an Executor of the estate of a deceased, and
  • a contract by a Guarantor of a debt (remember main purpose rule!).

NOTE: some professors add "S" for "Sale of goods", but that makes absolutely no sense. Goods are modernly governed by the UCC 2-201. If the question involves a sale of goods, discuss UCC 2-201, and don't discuss the Statute of Frauds. If the question is not about goods, discuss the Statute of Frauds and don't discuss goods!

4. DETERMINE THE TYPE OF CONTRACT QUESTION. There are 6 basic types of contract question. Sometimes a question combines two or three of the basic types. In any essay answer you should skip issues that do not merit discussion. For more information, see the lesson at   The 6 Types of Contract Questions. 

Remember, most law-school questions combine elements of each of the six types. For example, FORMATION and questions usually involve a BREACH, and a BREACH usually raises the question of DEFENSES. In the end the question of REMEDIES arises. So be prepared to start with a formation analysis, move on to a breach analysis and wrap it up with a discussion of remedies.

The structure of your answer should be as follows:

A. FORMATION QUESTIONS:  Follow this pattern.

  1. First look at each communication chronologically as a separate issue to see if it is an offer. 
  2. Second, after you find an offer, examine each following communication as a separate issue looking for an acceptance. It often is a rejection and counter-offer.
  3. Watch out for and apply the mailbox rules to revocations and rejections by mail/fax.
  4. When you get a valid acceptance to a valid offer, BINGO!, you have a contract. So the very next single issue is to determine its terms. 
  5. Don't discuss consideration, legal purpose or legal capacity of the parties unless the facts call for discussion.
  • Remember that consideration is a bargained for exchange.
  • No moral obligation. 
  • No prior consideration.
  • No promise qualified by a future decision to perform.

B. TERMS DISPUTES: These are three situations where the parties dispute the terms. Usually there is only one issue and it will be one of the following: 

  1. Is the contract too uncertain to be enforced? 
  • No quantity stated? (Not a UCC requirements or production contract!)
  • Parties uncertain? 
  • Uncertain as to time, price, subject matter? (Not UCC wheregap fillers apply!)
  1. Is the modification supported by adequate consideration? 
  • Prior obligation? 
  • This does not apply to UCC where no considerations is needed.
  1. Does the parole evidence rule bar extrinsic evidence of additional terms?  
  • Merger clause? 
  • Contract complete within the four corners of the document?
  • Remember the exceptions (mnemonic = DAM FOIL).
  1. Was timely performance an EXPRESS CONDITION (a material condition) or just a COVENANT (not a material condition)?

[For an explanation of express conditions versus covenants see Nailing the Bar's "Simple Contracts & UCC Outline"]

  1. Was personal satisfaction an EXPRESS CONDITION (a material condition that the buyer must actually be satisfied) or just a COVENANT (an expression of the implied condition inherent in all contracts that performance must be "reasonably satisfactory")?

C. ANTICIPATORY BREACH (a.k.a. anticipatory repudiation) and WAIVER OF THE BREACH: These situations are where one party says, "I don't think I can meet the deadline." Then the other party says, "Oh yeah? Well then the deal's off."  Follow this pattern:

  1. Look at each communication as a separate issue to see if it is a clear statement of intent not to perform at a future time. If a party says they don't "think" they will be able to perform in the future, that is expression of a doubt and not an expression of an intent. 
  2. The first party to clearly state an intent to not perform is the breaching party.
  3. When a party breaches the next issue is the effect of the anticipatory breach. It 1) excuses the non-breaching party from performance and it 2) accelerates the performance obligation of the breaching party.
  4. The next issue is whether the non-breaching party waives the breach by allowing the breaching party to continue performance under the contract.

D. DEFENSES. If there is a breach, go through each of the breaching party's defenses as separate issues:

  1. FIRST, was there an insufficient writing to satisfy the Statute of Frauds?
  2. Lack of consideration? Prior obligation? Moral obligation? Prior consideration? Gift promise?
  3. Illegal purpose? Does in pare delicto apply?
  4. Breaching party a minor or incapacitated? But, did they repudiate within time after removal of incapacity?
  5. Contract induced by fraud, duress, or because of mistake? Did the non-breaching party know, or should they have known that the other party was mistaken? 
  6. Performance impossible or purpose frustrated? Was it absolutely impossible? These situations violate IMPLIED MATERIAL CONDITIONS of the contract, causing it to fail.

[For an explanation of the IMPLIED MATERIAL CONDITIONS of every contract see Nailing the Bar's "Simple Contracts & UCC Outline".]

E. THIRD PARTY QUESTIONS. These are similar to DEFENSES and the issues raised are:

  1. Does the third party have standing to bring an action? Intended beneficiary?
  2. Was there an improper assignment of the contract? Notice not given?
  3. Was there an improper delegation of the contract? Personal services?

F. REMEDIES. Each of the remedies is a separate issue. Only discuss the issues that apply.

  1. If there was a valid contract, was there substantial performance? Benefit of bargain?
  2. Can the plaintiff obtain specific performance? Unique goods?
  3. Can the contract be enforced based on promissory estoppel? Injustice resulting?
  4. Can the party obtain rescission and restitution? Return to status quo?
  5. Should the damages be reduced because of a failure to mitigate?
  6. Are consequential damages called for? Cite Hadley v. Baxendale, and the mnemonic is CCCC.
  7. If there is NO VALID, ENFORCEABLE CONTRACT what EQUITABLE remedies are available to the parties? 

5. HOW MUCH TIME AND ANALYSIS? Follow the call of the question but otherwise spend about the same amount of time on each issue. If the call asks for discussion of "rights and remedies" be sure to spend some time discussing the remedies! 

6. PHRASE ISSUES FOR CONTRACT QUESTIONS AS FOLLOWS:

I like to see issues spelled out completely but your professor might not.

    1. Does the UCC govern?
    2. STATUTE OF FRAUDS require a writing?
    3. Letter of 5/1 an OFFER by Bob?
    4. Letter of 6/1 an OFFER by Sam?
    5. Letter of 7/1 a REJECTION AND COUNTEROFFER by Bob?
    6. Letter of 7/1 an ACCEPTANCE by Sam?
    7. TERMS of the agreement?
    8. PAROLE EVIDENCE RULE?
    9. MODIFICATION WITHOUT CONSIDERATION?
    10. ANTICIPATORY BREACH?
    11. BREACH?
    12. Duty to MITIGATE?
    13. WRITING satisfied?
    14. SUBSTANTIAL PERFORMANCE?
    15. SPECIFIC PERFORMANCE?
      16.

You must make it clear just what you think the issue is. For example, if there are multiple communications going back and forth between the parties and your issue is stated only as "OFFER?" how will the reader know which communication you mean?
